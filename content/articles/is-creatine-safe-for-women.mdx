Creatine has a reputation as a "men's" supplement, but it's one of the most
researched supplements in all of sports nutrition — and women are well represented
in the evidence base.

{/* COMPLIANCE: stick to "is associated with" / "may support". No disease claims. */}

## What creatine is

Creatine is a compound your body makes and also gets from foods like meat and fish.
In muscle, it helps regenerate energy during short, intense efforts. Supplementing
raises the amount stored in muscle. [^1]

## What the evidence says for women

For women, creatine monohydrate is associated with improvements in strength and
high-intensity performance when paired with resistance training. [^2] Research
interest has also grown around potential roles beyond the gym, though much of that
is still emerging and shouldn't be oversold.

A common worry is "bulking up." Creatine doesn't do that by itself. Early scale
changes are usually water held inside muscle cells, not fat gain.

## How to take it

- **Dose:** about 3–5 g per day of creatine monohydrate.
- **Loading is optional.** You can skip the loading phase; you'll just reach full
  muscle stores a bit more slowly.
- **Consistency beats timing.** Daily intake matters more than the exact time.

If you're pregnant, or have a kidney condition, talk to your clinician before
starting.
